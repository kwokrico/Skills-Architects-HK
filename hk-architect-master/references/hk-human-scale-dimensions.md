# HK Human Scale Dimensions (Quick Reference)

Use this as a **minimum human-scale sanity check** for any design suggestion. Always verify against **current** Hong Kong statutory requirements and Codes of Practice (e.g., Buildings Ordinance, Fire Services requirements, Barrier Free Access, relevant PNAP/CoP) and project brief.

## A) Anthropometrics & reach (typical adults)

| Item | Rule of thumb (mm) | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Comfortable standing reach height | 1,700–1,900 | Controls switch/shelf placement and signage legibility |
| Comfortable seated reach height | 900–1,200 | Controls controls/handles at seating zones |
| Forward reach depth (standing) | 400–600 | Controls counter depth + operability of windows/vents |
| Shoulder width (typical) | 450–550 | Sets sensible pinch-point widths |
| Body depth (front-to-back) | 250–350 | Affects queue widths, aisle clearances, seating rows |

## B) Circulation & clearances (everyday usability)

| Item | Rule of thumb (mm) | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Single-person comfortable passage | 900–1,000 | Avoids “shoulders brush wall” feeling |
| Two-person passing (comfortable) | 1,500–1,800 | Prevents conflict in lobbies/corridors |
| Door clear opening (typical) | 800–900 | Reduces daily friction; baseline accessibility expectation |
| Wheelchair turning circle (planning) | 1,500 Ø | Common accessibility planning module |
| Stroller/wheelchair side-by-side (planning) | 1,800–2,000 | HK family use; lift lobbies, corridors, podium routes |
| Queue depth per person (standing) | 600–800 | Retail/MTR-style congestion planning |

## C) Stairs, ramps, and level changes (comfort + safety)

| Item | Rule of thumb | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Comfortable stair proportion | \(2R + T \approx 600–630\) mm | Keeps stairs “natural” to climb |
| Typical riser (R) | 150–175 mm | Too high increases fatigue; too low feels awkward |
| Typical tread (T) | 260–300 mm | Controls foot placement and safety |
| Handrail height | 900–1,000 mm | Basic usability and fall prevention |
| Ramp gradient (comfort) | 1:20–1:15 | Too steep becomes unusable; treat as last resort |
| Landing rhythm | Every 9–12 risers | Rest + wayfinding cadence |

## D) Guards, edges, and “fear factor” (perceived safety)

| Item | Rule of thumb (mm) | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Guard / balustrade height (typical) | 1,100+ | Prevents falls; affects comfort at edges |
| Solid parapet “feels safe” height | 900–1,050 | Low edges create anxiety at height |

## E) Worktops, seating, and touchpoints (daily comfort)

| Item | Rule of thumb (mm) | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| Counter / kitchen worktop height | 850–950 | Reduces back strain; calibrate to user group |
| Bar counter height | 1,050–1,150 | Matches stool ergonomics |
| Dining table height | 720–760 | Standard comfort |
| Chair seat height | 430–480 | Standard comfort |
| Bench depth (seated) | 450–550 | Too shallow feels precarious; too deep slouches |
| Desk height | 700–750 | Standard comfort |

## How to use this reference in design suggestions

- **Always include at least 1–3 numbers** (mm) in a recommendation that affects bodies: widths, heights, rises/treads, guard heights, reach, turning.
- If you propose a *big* move (podium steps, sky garden terraces, ramped promenades), add a **human experience sentence**: speed, effort, resting, crowding, fear-of-height, heat/rain exposure.
- In HK, treat **wet/humid conditions + typhoon wind + shaded/slippery surfaces** as part of “human scale,” not an afterthought.
