# HK Fee Proposal Strategy
Supports architect-side fee bidding and scope definition with clear commercial boundaries and defensible change control.

---

## 1. Core Position
- Treat fee proposals as risk allocation documents, not only pricing submissions.
- Define deliverables, assumptions, exclusions, and review cycles before giving numbers.
- Price for the likely submission pathway (including revision risk), not the optimistic pathway.

---

## 2. Main Pricing Methods

### 2.1 Percentage of Construction Cost
- Typical when scope maturity is low or project size is expected to move.
- Formula: `Architect Fee = Construction Cost x Agreed Percentage`.
- Prefer clear cost basis: latest QS estimate, accepted cost plan stage, and what is included/excluded (fit-out, furniture, contingencies, statutory fees, etc.).

Pros:
- Scales with project complexity and size growth.
- Easier to explain as project value changes.

Watchpoints:
- Disputes happen when construction cost baseline is undefined.
- Late-stage value engineering can reduce fee unexpectedly unless floor protections are stated.

### 2.2 Lump Sum
- Suitable for well-defined scope with stable programme and limited unknowns.
- Set by deliverables + milestones + assumptions, not by arbitrary discounting.

Pros:
- Budget certainty for client.
- Simpler payment administration.

Watchpoints:
- High exposure if approvals are unstable or client brief evolves.
- Must clearly define what counts as additional services.

---

## 3. Scope Framing for Fee Bids
Minimum scope sections to include:
1. Project definition (site, use mix, gross floor area assumptions, procurement path).
2. Stage-by-stage services (concept, statutory submission, tender, site, closeout).
3. Coordination obligations (QS, RSE, MEP, specialist consultants).
4. Submission assumptions (number of BD/authority rounds included).
5. Programme assumptions and client response time assumptions.
6. Explicit exclusions and optional services.

---

## 4. Scope Creep Management
- Define "scope creep" as any requested effort beyond agreed baseline deliverables, revisions, or timeline assumptions.
- Use a change trigger list in the proposal so extra work is objectively identifiable.
- Keep instruction history and revision log from day one for evidence.

Common scope creep triggers:
- Increase in gross floor area or use-mix complexity.
- Multiple redesign cycles after design freeze.
- Additional authority liaison beyond agreed rounds.
- Programme compression requiring parallel rework.
- Late client brief changes affecting approved packages.

---

## 5. Additional Services Definition
Additional services should be pre-defined with pricing principles and examples.

Typical additional services (examples):
- Specialized lighting design integration beyond base architectural coordination.
- Extra BD re-submissions beyond included rounds.
- Major redesign after client brief change.
- Statutory strategy workshops outside base meetings.
- Enhanced BIM coordination, visuals, or tenant-fitout packages not in base scope.

Recommended structure:
- Define the trigger.
- Define the charging method (lump sum, hourly cap, or percentage add-on).
- Define approval process before commencement.

---

## 6. Fee Proposal Safeguards
- Include a fee validity period.
- Tie payment milestones to deliverable acceptance, not only dates.
- Include suspension and remobilization terms for prolonged client-side delays.
- Set a minimum fee floor if percentage-based method is adopted.
- State reimbursement policy for direct expenses and disbursements.

---

## 7. Quick Decision Guide
- Use **percentage-based fee** when cost and scope are still moving.
- Use **lump sum** when scope is fixed and approvals path is predictable.
- Use **hybrid model** (base lump sum + predefined additional services rates) when tender competitiveness is needed but risk remains high.

---

## 8. Practical Output Checklist
- Fee basis and cost baseline defined.
- Scope inclusions/exclusions explicit and testable.
- Included submission/revision rounds quantified.
- Additional services list and rates included.
- Variation instruction and approval protocol stated.
- Payment milestones and delay/suspension terms stated.

---

*Project teams should align this framework with appointment terms, procurement route, and Hong Kong contract practice on each live project.*
